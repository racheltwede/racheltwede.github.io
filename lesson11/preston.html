A Year of Insanity, but a Day to be Grateful

Article by Taylor Lawrence

It goes without saying that this last year has been a difficult one. The Coronavirus pandemic affected everyone in enormous ways, and as grief and worry often do, they have led to feelings of stress and anger. Furious debates still rage about what the United States government could have, or should have, done to lessen the pandemic's impact in our nation—and that's not even considering the huge amount of political and social discord we've seen over the past year. Fingers have been pointed, and I've heard more than one discouraged or heated conversation about the people of our country and the climate of skepticism and negativity that seems to have swept us under. With this in mind as we steadily approach this year's Fourth of July, I've heard some people wonder: "Do we really have a reason to celebrate, this year? Is our nation still worth celebrating?"

Call me naive, or maybe just or overly optimistic, but when somebody near me voices these doubts, it's all I can do to keep myself from taking them by the shoulders and answering them with a heartfelt "yes!"

The people who live here aren't perfect. It would be dangerous not to admit that. No matter your political beliefs, it's clear that our country has flaws, and that some of those flaws are a very big deal. Even so, as drive to work or sit in church or listen to conflicting opinions on the news, I can't help but feel grateful that I'm free to do those things at all. When I sit in my comfortable front room and listen to my children talk about what they learned at school, I feed amazed and overwhelmed that I've been blessed to live in a place where they can have that education.
